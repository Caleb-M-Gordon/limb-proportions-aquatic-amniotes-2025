Black	Dot	Alligator_mississippiensis	YPM_HERR_11754	Reptilia	Archosauromorpha	Recent	Non-flippered	Highly Aquatic	All extant crocodilians are amphibious predators that spend extensive time hunting and wading in the water, but also routinely bask, nest, and travel to some degree on land (Whitaker and Bass 1982; Ferguson 1985; Vitt and Caldwell 2014, ch. 19: Crocodylians; Murray et al. 2020). These species nevertheless span a continuum of semi-aquatic lifestyles, which differ intraspecifically, ontogenetically, and depending on weather and other external factors. In order to bin crocodilians by ecotype in an analysis that could plausibly encompass all other amniotes, we inevitably oversimplify the complex natural history of the clade. We try only to score them in a consistent way that broadly reflects their ecological history permits comparison to other amniotes. To that end, we describe our reasoning for these scores. We consider the brevirostrine species Osteolaemus tetraspis and Paleosuchus trigonatus the least aquatic crocodilians. O. tetraspis often inhabits sparsely inundated shallow inland streams and cave systems (Crocodile Specialist Group 1996; Vitt and Caldwell 2014, p. 332: Crocodylidae; Shirley et al. 2017), and it regularly hunts small terrestrial prey (Pauwels et al. 2007), which may make up the entirety of its adult diet in cave-dwelling populations (Shirley et al. 2017). Though quantitative comparisons of water and land habitation are sparse, one study found that some (but not all) captive O. tetraspis individuals spent the overwhelming majority of their time on land (Uwakaneme et al. 2004 [abstract])—a finding consistent with the highly terrestrial diet of some populations (Shirley et al. 2017). P. trigonatus is likewise often found about small inland streams in heavily forested areas (Medem 1958; Magnusson et al. 1987; Brochu 2001); adults consume primarily terrestrial prey (Jackson et al. 1974; Magnusson et al. 1987), and spens vast stretches of time on land, with one wild individual apparently residing under dry ground for 10 days (Magnusson and Lima 1990). These relatively terrestrial habits have led some authors to suggest a connection between terrestriality and brevirostry (Kälin 1933, cited in Medem 1958; Brochu 2001). However, the closely related P. palpebrosus, which is more conspicuously brevirostrine than its congener (Medem 1958), resides about larger water bodies and consumes relatively less terrestrial prey (Magnusson et al. 1987), casting doubt on this suggestion. Regardless, these species still typically spend extended periods of time in the water and have at least partly aquatic diets. As a result, we consider P. trigonatus and O. tetraspis Moderately Aquatic, with equal-to-subequal regions of niche space and habitation time devoted to land and water. In contrast, we consider P. palpebrosus and all other extant crocodilian species Highly Aquatic, as most individuals seem to spend the majority of their time in the water, coming onto land only to bask and nest. Within this single bin of “Highly Aquatic” crocodilians, we stress the existence of a finer continuum. In particular, we might well consider gavialids and the Saltwater Crocodile more aquatic than the other species sorted within this bin. Perhaps because they descend from marine ancestors (Wilberg et al. 2019), the gavialids G. gangeticus and T. schlegelli have longirostrine snouts often treated as specializations for hunting fish, though their diet is really more varied (Whitaker and Basu 1982). And while T. schlegelli occupies lentic marsh waters (Staniewicz 2018), G. gangeticus resides in deep, fast-moving rivers, seldom (if ever) moves far from the water’s edge, often remains submerged during nest attendance, and has more limited mobility on land than other modern crocodilians (Whitaker and Bass 1982; Vitt and Caldwell 2014: Gavialidae, p. 548; Choudhary et al. 2017; Murray et al. 2020). As a result, G. gangeticus is often considered the most aquatic modern crocodilian (Vitt and Caldwell 2014: Gavialidae, p. 548). In a similar vein, the  Crocodylus porosus may be the most active swimmer among modern crocodilians, often making extended (>1000 km) journeys over open ocean (Fukuda et al. 2019; Spennerman 2020). As a result, one might draw finer ecotypic distinctions among the many less terrestrial crocodilians. For this study, given that gavialids, C. porosus, and all their potentially less aquatic counterparts seem to spend the majority of their time in the water, but periodically return to land to nest, rest, and bask, we consider them all Highly Aquatic. In brief, we treat O. tetraspis and P. trigonatus as Moderately Aquatic, and all other species as Highly so.
